Glossary

Actor
The lived sense of “I” moving through the world. The actor is not an illusion to be destroyed, but a role to be recognized. When seen clearly, the actor relaxes and performs without confusion.
See also: Role, Mask, Play.

Advaita Vedanta
A non-dual philosophical tradition from India asserting that reality is ultimately one, without separation. Advaita does not aim at becoming enlightened, but at recognizing that separation was never real.
See also: Non-duality, Vedanta, Universal Consciousness.

Awareness
That which notices experience without effort. Awareness does not come and go; only attention shifts. It remains when seeking pauses.

Bardo
In Tibetan Buddhism, an intermediate state between forms or identities. In this book, bardo refers to any in-between moment where certainty dissolves and something new has not yet formed.
See also: Intermission, Return.

Bodhisattva
In Buddhism, one who awakens yet chooses to remain engaged with the world. The bodhisattva does not escape the play, but returns to it with compassion and clarity.
See also: Return, Enlightenment.

Buddhism
A tradition oriented toward the alleviation of suffering through direct insight into impermanence, non-self, and interdependence. Here, Buddhism is approached as lived inquiry rather than belief.
See also: Samsara, Bodhisattva.

Bow
A moment of acknowledgment when striving ends. The bow is not submission, but recognition—life recognizing itself without resistance.

Consciousness
The field within which all experience appears. Thoughts, sensations, identities, and worlds arise in consciousness, but consciousness itself does not arise.

Enlightenment
Not a permanent emotional state or final achievement. Enlightenment is the recognition of what is already present, followed by the ordinary responsibility of living from that recognition.
See also: Return, Universal Consciousness.

Flow
Action without internal resistance. When effort relaxes, movement continues naturally. Flow is cooperation, not control.
See also: Wu Wei.

Forgetting
The necessary function that allows the role to be played sincerely. Forgetting enables immersion. Awakening does not remove forgetting; it loosens attachment to it.
See also: Remembering.

Identity
A practical construction made of memory, habit, and story. Identity functions well until it is mistaken for essence.

Intermission
A pause where nothing is added and nothing is removed. Intermission is not an interruption of the play, but the play noticing itself.
See also: Bardo, Silence.

Jhana
In Buddhist meditation, a state of deep absorption and stillness. Jhana is not enlightenment itself, but a condition in which clarity becomes possible.
See also: Shamatha.

Kabbala / Kabbalah
A mystical tradition within Judaism exploring the structure of reality through symbolic and contemplative means. Kabbala emphasizes participation in divine unfolding rather than escape from the world.
See also: Tikkun.

Lila
A Sanskrit term meaning “divine play.” Lila describes existence as spontaneous expression rather than problem to be solved.
See also: Play.

Mask
The personality assumed for expression and navigation. Masks are not deceptions; they are tools. Suffering arises when a mask is mistaken for the face beneath.
See also: Persona.

Maya
Often translated as illusion, Maya refers not to falseness but to appearance mistaken for absolute reality. Maya is convincing, functional, and incomplete.
See also: Phenomenon.

Non-duality
The recognition that reality is not divided into subject and object, self and world. Non-duality is not a belief, but a shift in perception.
See also: Advaita Vedanta.

Noumenon
That which exists beyond sensory perception or conceptual description. The noumenon is not known directly, but inferred when appearances are seen through.
See also: Phenomenon.

Persona
The social face presented to the world. Persona allows participation and communication, but is not the totality of being.
See also: Mask, Role.

Phenomenon
Anything that appears to perception or experience. Phenomena are real as appearances, but not absolute in nature.
See also: Maya, Noumenon.

Play
The total movement of existence—serious and light, tragic and comic—occurring without a final script. Capital-P Play refers to life itself.
See also: Lila, Stage.

Pratyahara
In yogic philosophy, the withdrawal of attention from sensory objects. Pratyahara is not suppression, but a turning inward that reveals stillness.
See also: Yoga.

Remembering
The quiet recognition of what was never lost. Remembering is not recalling information, but recognizing presence.
See also: Forgetting.

Return
The movement back into ordinary life after insight. The return is not regression, but embodiment—clarity lived rather than preserved.
See also: Bodhisattva.

Role
A temporary configuration of behavior and belief. Roles enable participation without defining essence.
See also: Actor.

Sahaja Samadhi
A term describing natural, effortless awareness integrated into daily life. Sahaja samadhi is not trance, but ease within activity.
See also: Return.

Samsara
The cycle of seeking, dissatisfaction, and repetition driven by misidentification. Samsara ends not when circumstances change, but when the relationship to them does.
See also: Enlightenment.

Shamatha
A meditation practice cultivating calm attention and stability. Shamatha steadies the mind so insight can arise.
See also: Jhana.

Silence
Not the absence of sound, but the absence of resistance. Silence remains when commentary falls away.

Stage
The field in which experience appears. The stage is not separate from the actor or the audience; it holds them all.
See also: Play.

Synchronicity
Meaningful coincidence that appears when resistance softens. Synchronicity does not prove control, but reveals alignment.

Taoism
A tradition emphasizing harmony with the natural movement of life. Taoism values simplicity, spontaneity, and alignment over force.
See also: Wu Wei.

Theatron
The ancient Greek term for “a place of seeing.” Theatron reminds us that theater, like consciousness, exists so something may be witnessed.

Tikkun
In Kabbalistic thought, the repair or integration of the world through conscious participation. Tikkun does not fix existence; it heals relationship.
See also: Kabbala.

Transcendentalism
A philosophical movement valuing direct experience of truth over doctrine or authority. Transcendentalism trusts intuition as a valid form of knowing.

Universal Consciousness
The recognition that awareness is not personal or owned. Universal consciousness is not entered; it is shared.
See also: Non-duality.

Vedanta
A family of Indian philosophical teachings exploring the nature of reality and self. Vedanta asks not how to improve the self, but what the self truly is.
See also: Advaita Vedanta.

Vikshepa
Mental distraction or scattering. Vikshepa describes attention pulled outward by habit rather than intention.
See also: Shamatha.

Witness
The observing presence that remains untouched by what it observes. The witness is intimate without being entangled.

Wu Wei
A Taoist term meaning “non-doing” or “effortless action.” Wu Wei describes behavior that arises in harmony with circumstances, without force or resistance.
See also: Flow.

Yoga
Originally meaning “union.” Yoga refers to practices that quiet fragmentation and reveal wholeness. Yoga is not posture, but integration.
See also: Pratyahara.
