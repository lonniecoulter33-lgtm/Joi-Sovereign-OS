Chapter One: The Dance of Lila
Who Am I, and Why Am I Alive?
There is a question that arises. Before words, before memory, before you put on the mask and form your identity, the answer is known. But along the way, it becomes forgotten.

It arises quietly, sometimes without words, sometimes in the form of a subtle curiosity. Why am I here? Not merely in this room, or in this body, or in this particular life, but here at all. Here, as awareness itself.

Often, this question does not announce itself loudly. It lies beneath daily activity, presenting itself through ordinary moments. It may appear while washing dishes, while staring out a window, or while waking in the middle of the night with no clear reason why. There is a deeper question beneath all others, and it is not philosophical in the way arguments are philosophical. It arrives quietly, often without words, sometimes as wonder, sometimes as unease. It does not ask how to live or what to believe. It is something more primitive and more difficult to hold: Why is there anything at all?

Not why the world is arranged this way, or why events unfold as they do, but why there is experience rather than absence, appearance rather than silence. Why this moment, rather than none? Why does existence insist on happening?

This question has been approached carefully for thousands of years, not because it is dangerous, but because it cannot be solved without being reduced. To answer it too quickly is to replace it with an idea. And yet, to never feel it is to miss something essential. The question does not arise in thought alone; it appears in the body, in the weight of being here, in the strange intimacy of existing at all. You do not think your way into it; you find yourself already inside it.

Who is it that is here?

It is not asking for a name, a history, or a role. It is not satisfied by personality, memory, or intention. Even the idea of a self feels provisional when examined closely, as though it were something assembled rather than something discovered. And yet, something is undeniably present. You cannot locate it precisely. You cannot step outside of it to examine it. Whatever you point to—body, thought, emotion, awareness—already feels like it belongs to the world that is being questioned, not to the one doing the questioning.

 Vedanta approached it by asking, “What remains when everything observable is set aside?” Buddhism approached it by asking whether anything could be found at all. Zen approached it by refusing to stabilize the question long enough for an answer to form. Different approaches; the same stirring of reality.

Perhaps the question Who am I? is inseparable from the question Why is there experience at all? Perhaps they are two ways the same mystery makes itself felt. The fact remains, however, that beneath the roles, the stories, and the momentum of living, there is something quietly astonishing about the fact that any of this can be happening—and that you are here to notice it, without yet knowing what that means.

The play has already begun.

The question is not whether you may be part of it. The question is: who is it that is here, asking why there is a play at all? Consider that out of all the possible moments that could have been, out of the vast stretch of what we experience as time, you find yourself reading these words now. You have not already lived and vanished forever. You have not remained unborn, waiting endlessly to appear. Somehow, and mysteriously, you are present. You find that you are actually here, in the now, existing. Pretty strange, isn't it?

No committee approved your arrival, and there was no cosmic schedule that was consulted. There was no pre-rehearsal. And yet here you are, fully cast, mid-scene. Existing. This fact is so ordinary that it is rarely examined and often goes unacknowledged, and yet it is the most astonishing thing about you.

Just ponder this for a moment. If existence were a coin tossed through eternity, the odds of landing on this moment are improbable, if not just downright impossible. However, despite all the odds, here you are. Breathing and reading; fully aware and alive. Awareness did not just happen one day within the scope of what we consider eternal time. Awareness began when eternity began, and it will end when eternity ceases to be eternal.

Take a moment to feel the weightlessness of that statement. Not as an idea, but as an understanding you already innately know. There is something in you that has never been rushed by time. Awareness itself is often taken for granted. It feels so ordinary, so common, that it disappears into familiarity. We often forget that we are aware. Consider this fact: nothing else you experience—no thought, no sensation, no memory—could appear without it.

Awareness is the quiet condition that allows everything else to be known. It is not an object in experience; it is the space in which experience happens. Like a stage so familiar that the actors forget it exists, awareness remains unseen precisely because it never leaves. Awareness is the stage in which all of life becomes manifested. Everything we perceive, we do on the stage of awareness. Before we ask what life is for, it should be worth noticing that life is already happening, effortlessly and without your instruction.

Take another moment, not to analyze, but to notice. Notice that this page is being read. Reading is occurring. Words appear. Meaning forms. Attention moves. And yet, none of this requires effort to be known. It is just happening.

There is a peculiar recognition in this noticing. Nothing mystical needs to occur. Nothing dramatic needs to unfold. The simplicity of it is the revelation. All of this can be known, not through effort and not by any form of analysis. Not even by thought. It is simply evident. It is you, here and now, reading these lines. The same "You" who has always been present. Long before you learned your name. Long before you were taught what you were supposed to be.

 I offer to you, in the pages that follow, a glimpse into the illusory aspect of life. I offer you questions that will guide you to the answers of what lies beneath the very mask reality itself wears. We have been here before. Across cultures and centuries, human beings have sensed that something about existence is both deeply convincing and subtly unreal, like a performance so immersive that the audience forgets they are seated in a theatre.

In Hinduism, this is called Maya. In Buddhism, Samsara. These are Sanskrit terms for this physical incarnation within the endless cycles of recurring existence that we believe to be our only true reality. This is the great divine play, Lila—the eternal dance of Shiva. This dance is so convincing that the dancer forgets the choreography, forgets the stage, and forgets the watching presence altogether.

What is it that looks out from behind the eyes? No need to answer just yet; simply notice that the question points rather than asks. There is nothing to instantly grasp. Just experience what the question implies: Something is always present. Even between thoughts. In Tibetan Buddhism, the space between two thoughts is a Bardo—any point of existence between two other points of existence.

Close your eyes and sit in silence. Look into that space between your thoughts and notice what is there. It does not arrive with the words, nor depart when they end. It is the Silent Observer, the Witness to the stage performance that contains every gesture, every interaction, and every passing moment of your life. It has never been absent. It has never died. It was not just born when you thought you came into this world. This Witness does not applaud or boo. It does not critique the performance. It simply allows the play to unfold.

Can you imagine where you would be if you ceased to exist? Try to think of what it is like to not be "You" just for a moment. Can you? Notice how thought reaches for an image, a memory, or a concept, and finds nothing. The mind stumbles here, not because the question is absurd, but because the Witness cannot reference an experience of non-existence.

Your body moves through the scene. Your persona speaks, reacts, remembers, and anticipates. Thoughts arise and dissolve as your character meets the shifting landscape of phenomena. But all of this is witnessed by something that does not move. No matter how hard you try, you cannot escape yourself.

The Witness does not move with the movements. It does not think the thoughts that announce themselves as "You." It listens to the sounds thoughts make as they pass through, the way one might listen to the wind moving through trees. Heard, but not followed. Each thought flutters like a butterfly’s wings—brief, delicate, restless. Arising, shimmering, dissolving. But the Observer does not flutter. It does not rush. It only watches.

Always present.

Like a child absorbed in a film, you are fully immersed in the story while never entering the screen. Moved, but not consumed. Long before this body appeared, something was already present. Long after it dissolves, something will remain. Not as a memory or a continuation of a personality, but as the same simple knowing that is reading these words now. Continuing without identity. Always there.

This is not an idea to adopt. It is not a belief to defend. You do not need to accept it for it to be true. This is the most intimate and fundamental fact of your existence. You may have learned to call yourself many things—a name, a belief, a profession, an emotion. These are roles, costumes worn by the body and mind as they move through this world of Maya. They are necessary and convincing, and they help the story function. But beneath every role is the same unmoving presence, unchanged by success or failure, untouched by joy or grief.

The stage does not celebrate or mourn. Like an actor changing masks across scenes, the character shifts while the stage remains. The actor doesn't disappear.

Ask yourself this question: If you had all of eternity in which to exist, what would you do? Would you rush towards some idea of a final Nirvana, never to live as a human being again? Never to feel love or pain again? Or would you return, again and again, slipping back into the costume, letting the lights rise, letting the world convince you once more?

Perhaps you would forget yourself completely, so the story could feel real. So laughter could surprise you. So love would captivate you once again. Perhaps you would remember, briefly, just enough to smile at the seriousness of it all. Then you would step back into the role, letting the world convince you once again—because without forgetting, there could be no surprise, no intimacy, no genuine emotion. No play.

This is what I would want to do with eternity. I wouldn't want to stop existing or find some final resting place. I would want to keep coming back to experience all the thrills that being a conscious being offers. Remembering and forgetting are not opposites; they are partners. Each gives meaning to the other. Yin and yang. Duality out of oneness.

The mystery of being alive is not something to solve. It is something already happening, and you can't stop it. Nothing essential is missing. Presence needs no explanation. Before the first thought, there was already knowing. Before the question, there was already an answer that did not need words. You did not arrive here. You noticed you were already present.

Who is it that remembers this? Can an actor escape the stage without leaving the play?
