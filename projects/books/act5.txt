Chapter Five: Stillness Is a Doorway 


Imagine a walkie-talkie. To send a message, you press the button first, and then you speak. 
While the button is pressed, the device transmits, but it cannot receive any transmissions while sending a message.



 Only when the button is released can another transmission be heard.



Try it, let go of the button.


The simplicity of the mechanism is part of its brilliance. There is no ambiguity built into its function; it either speaks or listens, never debating which it is doing. 



Take a moment to process this information. 


This simple mechanism reveals something easily overlooked: transmission and reception cannot happen at the same time. One excludes the other. The device is either speaking or listening, never both at once. There is no overlap, no compromise, and no partial reception while transmission continues.

The mind works the same way. It is a walkie-talkie.

Each thought is a transmission. 
Each idea, memory, plan, or judgment is a message sent outward and, at the same time, heard inward. 
The strange thing is this: the mind is both the sender and the receiver when engaged in thinking and conceptualization.



Notice how easy it is to understand this without effort. You don’t have to do anything to know it; the knowing arrives on its own.

This points to something deeper: understanding itself does not require any strain. It arises naturally when nothing interferes. For a moment, notice the simple fact of being here before any attempt to change it. 





Stop. 







Witness the broadcast. 







You speak, and you are the only one who listens. 






Can you hear yourself?








There is no second party involved; no external audience.

The dialogue is entirely internal, looping endlessly back upon itself. You only hear yourself. 
You are sending messages to yourself. And as long as the button is pressed, no other signal can come through.


Go ahead and release the button.



What happens?



In the space between letting the button go and pushing it with the next thought, did you notice anything?







Try it again, release the button.








Thought returns, but not after a clear gap, a measurable mark of time.

This loop becomes so familiar that it feels like a form of companionship. 
It feels like continuity. 
It feels as if it could only be this way and not any other way. 
Yet it is only an echo.

The pressing of the button does not require effort. It happens automatically, habitually, without instruction. The same goes for not pressing the button, which also does not require effort. However, the mind presses the button constantly. Thought follows thought without pause. Even silence is filled with commentary. Even rest is analyzed.

The transmission never stops long enough to notice that something else has been waiting to speak. 
To get through the channel you have jammed up. 


To reach you.



This constant broadcasting becomes the background hum of existence. It is rarely questioned and never interrupted.




Stop. 



Did you notice the button being released?



This activity feels so normal because it has been continuous for so long. The background noise of thinking becomes indistinguishable from awareness itself. This is what Advaita Vedanta points to when it says the mind mistakes itself for the Self. The signal becomes so familiar that the receiver forgets there is a channel at all. And so no new messages are ever heard.


Locate a sound in the room. 






Now, think about tomorrow. 







Notice how the sound becomes a 'background' as you transmit. 







Now, stop thinking. 







The moment the thought stops, the sound rushes back to the foreground. 



The 'receiver' just clicked back on."



As long as the mind is talking and producing thought, replaying experiences, and searching for sensation, it cannot listen. The channel is busy; the signal is occupied.







Stop, and this time listen to the space between the thoughts.






 Take this moment and notice the simple fact of being here before any attempt to change it.

 Nothing needs to be done.


Just be aware.

The inability to hear is not a failure. It is a mechanical fact: transmission blocks reception. This is why stillness is so very essential in all meditation practices and why silence comes from doing nothing. Because only when there is silence can you actually hear all that is to be heard. Stillness is not the absence of experience; it is the absence of interference.

This distinction matters because most attempts at stillness unknowingly become another form of effort—another role the actor performs to arrive somewhere else. 

The mind tries to quiet itself, the body tries to relax, and attention tries to hold still. And in trying, movement is reintroduced. The effort to stop becomes more noise.

Stillness is not produced. It is carefully uncovered through restraining from any subtle movements of the body and then the mind. 
This is not a practice to master, but it is a condition that remains after the understanding of effort settles in. 
You don't need to perfect anything; you only need to do no-thing.

Stillness is not something added to the mind; it is what remains when the activity of transmission pauses.



When the mind stops producing messages, the button is finally released. 



And in that moment, something else becomes audible.


Can you hear it?


Not louder than thought, it is much quieter—so quiet that it is often overlooked because it does not compete with the presence of activity. 

This isn't the voice of the persona, the character rehearsing lines, defending identity, or managing the story.


It is quieter than that.
It does not argue or persuade; it does not present itself as a thought. 
It does not arrive with language or images. 


It simply knows.

When the body rests, it does not disappear; it is simply no longer being managed. This knowing does not announce itself as special. It feels ordinary, extremely intimate, and very familiar.



It is almost too quiet to notice.

This knowing is immediate, however, and complete within itself. Remember that no-thing is needed to bring it into your awareness. Stillness must settle in and be allowed to pervade one's awareness.

Not concentrating.
Not focusing. 
Not directing attention. 
Even now, as you read those words, notice whether the body subtly tries to do something, to aim, to hold, or to grasp. 

See that reflex, and let it dissolve into effortlessness.

Notice the simple fact of being here before any attempt to change it.


.Any form of effort presses the button again. 



To try is to press the button.

Even a subtle effort is still an effort; the desire to listen creates interference. The moment the mind reaches for something, it resumes transmitting.

Some meditation practices teach single-point focus as a tool to help one get to one step away from doing nothing. Remember, single-point concentration is still transmission. 


Trying to listen to silence is still an activity of the mind that produces more noise. 


Searching for silence only generates more sound through the effort of the mind searching.

Any desire only leads to the search for fulfillment or ends with non-fulfillment, never to stillness. Never to silence. Never to the higher states of realization. This is why transcendental traditions emphasize effortlessness. To transcend is to go beyond, not through effort but by the lack of it.

Not because effort is wrong, but because effort belongs to the realm of doing. What you are looking for here is prior to all doing.

Silence and stillness of the mind cannot be produced by any form of activity. It appears only when all forms of activity cease, and also the desire for any form of activity. You cannot look for silence; it cannot be found. To be found implies being once lost, and silence is never lost. It is only momentarily put aside so that the mind can fixate on that which it seeks.

To become aware of the silence that is always there, you can only stop creating the noise that obscures it. This is why the stillness of the body matters as a preliminary position to hold before any real progress is made.

Movement generates sensation.


 Sensation invites processing. 


Processing produces thought. 


Thought presses the button. 



The system is integrated; body and mind do not function independently. Activity in one echoes in the other.

When the body truly rests, stimulus decreases. The mind has less to interpret, less to manage, and less to consume. 
This is why ancient practices like Buddhist shamatha, yogic pratyahara, and ceremonial stillness in Kabbalah all begin by allowing the body to be completely supported and unmoving, as though it no longer needs supervision. The body learns, slowly, that nothing is being asked of it.

The nervous system begins to settle. 
All urgency fades, and the need to control slips away. 
Attention no longer has to patrol the environment.

Attention can only focus on one thing at a time. 


And so it is that when the urge to move fades, the urge to think often fades with it. Not because thought is suppressed, but because it is no longer required for processing.

Stillness is not forced. 
It is allowed to be in your attention. 
By doing nothing, the conditions for noise do not arise. 
The mind is not suppressed; it simply has nothing urgent to say or do. 

Activity ceases to happen.

There is no struggle involved, no resistance—just the absence of pressure.



Let go.





 Stop doing. 





Just be. 



Aware.




Feel the button no longer pressed.





Silence appears not as emptiness, but as presence pervading throughout everything. This silence is not the absence of sound. The world continues; birds call, the river moves, breath flows. What changes is our relationship to the movement of the world, to phenomena.

Before any method appears, before any instruction is followed, there is already a natural state of rest beneath activity. It exists before thought, before sensation, prior even to the idea of a self attempting to be still. Buddhist practice points to this directly, stating that the mind is already clear, already luminous. What obscures it is not disturbance itself, but the clinging to disturbance.

There comes a moment when even the movement toward transcendence grows quiet. Not because it has failed, and not because it has succeeded, but because it has carried attention as far as effort can take it. 

What remains is not another state to enter, but a different relationship to what is already present. Stillness is often misunderstood as something to be achieved, a condition brought about through discipline or technique. But when it appears naturally, it does not feel constructed; it feels uncovered. It is as though something that was always here has simply been relieved of interference.

This stillness is not the absence of thought. Thoughts may continue to arise. Sensations continue. The world does not withdraw. What changes is the way experience is held. 

Attention no longer feels compelled to move. It rests because there is nowhere else it needs to go.

In this resting, a different quality of knowing becomes apparent. It is not analytical or comparative. It does not divide experience into subject and object, observer and observed. Things are simply present, without the added requirement of being interpreted immediately—a priori knowledge derived without experience.

The body remains the body, the personality remains intact, and preferences do not vanish. The self does not dissolve into abstraction. What stops is identification with constant motion—the assumption that awareness must always be doing something to be alive. Stillness reveals that awareness does not depend on activity.

In deep quiet, the sense of time often thins. 


Time seems to be without a beginning or an end.


Not because time has stopped, but because the markers that usually measure it—anticipation, recollection, urgency—have lost their grip. Moments do not pile up; they open.

This state is familiar to many.

It appears spontaneously in nature, in moments of awe, in absorption, in grief, or in love.

 It is what meditation traditions learned to stabilize, not to create. 

And yet, even here, nothing needs to be held. Stillness comes and goes. It is not a permanent condition and does not elevate one moment over another. It simply shows that beneath the many configurations of mind you move through each day, there is a dimension that does not require movement to be present. Knowing this changes nothing immediately—and yet, it changes everything.

Once stillness is recognized as natural rather than manufactured, it ceases to be sought. And when it is no longer sought, it becomes available in ways effort could never produce.

Stillness begins not when sensation stops, but when resistance ends. 


The body continues to perceive; sounds arrive, pressure is felt, and the breath moves. None of this prevents stillness. 
What prevents stillness is the reflex to manage experience, to correct it, guide it, or comment on it—the processing of what the body perceives.

Children understand this instinctively, lying on the floor and allowing attention to rest where it lands without trying to meditate. To pass through this doorway consciously, the body must feel safe enough to stop correcting and adjusting. Once the body is supported and requires no muscular effort, it no longer needs attention and begins to settle on its own.

As the body stops asking for direction, attention often turns to the breath. 



Do you notice your breath?





Where is the 'you' that is breathing? 


Is it in the chest? 



Or is the chest simply a sensation appearing in the same space as this book?



Eventually, awareness remains present while the breath resumes its natural movement; it breathes itself. 


You witness this occurrence as something happening rather than something you are doing. 

Awareness is maintained while breathing happens, as if the mind is finally free from the task.


Awareness is always the foundation for all things to be known.


You do not have awareness; awareness has  'you' in it.



As stillness deepens, the body may fall away from reference, creating the possibility of release.
This is not a departure, but a state where the need to process input vanishes and awareness remains. 

There is no longer a need to be drawn to the sensations of the body; the sense of occupying a body dissolves.



Formlessness. Infinity. Eternity.


This is lucidity without an object—consciousness knowing itself. 
It becomes obvious that the body is not required for being; it is a vehicle, useful and temporary, entered for a journey and exited when movement is no longer needed.

When the mind rests, and the button is finally released, life is seen exactly as it has always been: empty of struggle, full of presence. 

There is no revelation, just a quiet familiarity, as if returning to a place never truly left. 




Silence does not wait for you to find it; when speaking stops, we realize it never left.




When no actor speaks, what is heard? 









Who is it that listens?
