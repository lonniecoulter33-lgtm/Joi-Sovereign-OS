
Chapter One
The Dance of Lila


Who Am I, and Why Am I Alive?

There is a question that arises.
Before words, before memory, before you put on the mask and form your identity, the answer is known, but along the way, it becomes forgotten.

 It arises quietly, sometimes without words, sometimes in the form of a subtle curiosity.
Why am I here?

Not merely in this room, or in this body, or in this particular life — but here at all.
Here, as awareness itself.

Often, this question does not announce itself loudly. It lies beneath daily activity, presenting itself through ordinary moments. It may appear while washing dishes, while staring out a window, while waking in the middle of the night with no clear reason why. 

Consider out of all the possible moments that could have been, out of the vast stretch of what we experience as time, you find yourself reading these words now. You have not already lived and vanished forever. You have not remained unborn, waiting endlessly to appear. Somehow, and mysteriously, you are present.
You are here in the now existing. Pretty strange, isn't it?

No committee approved your arrival, and there was no cosmic schedule that was consulted. There was no pre-rehearsal. And yet here you are, fully cast, mid-scene. Existing.

This fact is so ordinary that it is rarely examined and often goes unacknowledged, and yet it is the most astonishing thing about you.

Just ponder this for a moment.

If existence were a coin tossed through eternity, the odds of landing on this moment are improbable, if not just downright impossible. However despite all the odds, here you are. Breathing and reading fully aware and alive.
Awareness did not just happen one day within the scope of what we consider eternal time. Awareness began when eternity began, and it will end when eternity ceases to be eternal.

Take a moment to feel the weightlessness of that statement. Not as an idea, but as an understanding you already innately know. There is something in you that has never been rushed by time.

Awareness itself is often taken for granted. It feels so ordinary, so common, that it disappears into familiarity. We often forget that we are aware. Consider this fact that nothing else you experience — no thought, no sensation, no memory — could appear without it. Awareness is the quiet condition that allows everything else to be known.
It is not an object in experience; it is the space in which experience happens.

Like a stage so familiar that the actors forget it exists, awareness remains unseen precisely because it never leaves. Awareness is the stage in which all of life becomes manifested. Everything we perceive we do in the stage of awareness.

Before we ask what life is for, it should be worth noticing that life is already happening — effortlessly and
without your instruction.

Take another moment, not to analyze, but to notice.

Notice that this page is being read.
Reading is occurring.

Words appear. Meaning forms. Attention moves.
And yet, none of this requires effort to be known.

It is just happening.

There is a peculiar recognition in this noticing. Nothing mystical needs to occur. Nothing dramatic needs to unfold. The simplicity of it is the revelation.

All of this is known.
Not through effort and not by any form of analysis.
Not even by thought.

It is just simply evident.

It is you, here and now, reading these lines.
The same you who has always been present.

Long before you learned your name.
Long before you were taught what you were supposed to be.

I present to you in the pages that follow a glimpse into the illusionary aspect of life. Painted from my perspective using metaphors from Greek theatre and Taoism mixed with esoteric teachings from eastern philosophies like Hinduism and Buddhism ,all brought together in a westernized format. I offer you questions that will guide you to the answers of what lies beneath the mask reality wears. Consider this, we have been here before.

Across cultures and centuries, human beings have sensed that something about existence is both deeply convincing and subtly unreal — like a performance so immersive that the audience forgets they are seated in a theatre.

In Hinduism, this is referred to as Maya, Lila and is often called Samsara in Buddhism. All of which are Sanskrit terms for this physical incarnation within the endless cycles of recurring existence that we believe to be our only true reality. This is the divine play, the great dance of Shiva.

This is a dance so convincing that the dancer forgets the choreography, forgets the stage, forgets the watching presence altogether.

What is it that looks out from behind the eyes? What is it that hears the faint sound of pages turning, the subtle rhythm of breath, the distant movement of the world?
No need to answer just yet, simply notice that the question points rather than asks.

Questions like this are not meant to be solved. They are doorways. Fingers pointing toward something already here.

But what is it pointing to? And who is doing the pointing?

 There is nothing to grasp. Nothing to resolve. Just experience what the question implies.
Something is always present.
Even between thoughts. In Tibetan Buddhism, the space between two thoughts is a Bardo. Any point of existence between two other points of existence.

Close your eyes and sit in silence. Take notice of what is there, present. Look into that space between your thoughts and notice what is there.

It does not arrive with the words, nor depart when they end. It is called the silent observer — the witness to the stage performance that contains every gesture, every interaction, every passing moment of your life.
It has never been absent. It has never died and was not just born when you thought you came into this world.

This witness does not applaud or boo. It does not critique the performance. It simply allows the play to unfold.

Can you imagine where you would be if you ceased to exist? Try to think of what it is like to not be you just for a moment, can you?

Notice how thought reaches for an image, a memory, a concept — and finds nothing. The mind stumbles here, not because the question is flawed, but because the witness cannot reference an experience of non-existence. Your body moves through the scene. Your persona speaks, reacts, remembers, and anticipates. Thoughts arise and dissolve as your character meets the shifting landscape of phenomena.
Sensations register and emotions color the scene.

And yet all of this is witnessed by something that does not move.

No matter how hard you try, you cannot escape yourself.

The witness does not move with the movements. It does not think the thoughts that announce themselves as you. It listens to the sounds thoughts make as they pass through, the way one might listen to the wind moving through trees.
Heard, but not followed. Always there.

Each thought flutters like a butterfly’s wings — brief, delicate, restless.
Arising, shimmering, dissolving.

But the observer does not flutter.
It does not rush.

It only watches. Always there.

Like a child absorbed in a film, fully present to the story while never entering the screen.
Moved, but not consumed.

Long before this body appeared, something was already present.
Before memory could form.

Long after it dissolves, something will remain.
Not as a memory. Not as a continuation of a personality. But as the same simple knowing that is reading these words now.
Continuing without identity. Always there.

This is not an idea to adopt. It is not a belief to defend and it requires no agreement.
You do not need to accept it for it to be true.

This is the most intimate and fundamental fact of your existence. 

You may have learned to call yourself many things — a name, a belief, a profession, an emotion. These are roles, costumes worn by the body and mind as they move through this world of Maya. Necessary, convincing, and often taken to be final as they help the story function.

But beneath every role is the same unmoving presence, unchanged by success or failure, untouched by joy or grief.
The stage does not celebrate or mourn.

Like an actor changing masks across scenes, the character shifts while the stage remains.
And the stage does not age. The actor remains.

The stage is never troubled by the drama it holds.

Ask yourself this question.

If you had all of eternity in which to exist, what would you do?

Would you rush towards some idea of heaven or final nirvana? Never to live as a human being again? Never to feel love or pain again?

Or would you return — again and again — slipping back into the costume, letting the lights rise, letting the world convince you once more?

Perhaps you would forget yourself completely, so the story could feel real.
So laughter could surprise you. So love would captivate you once again.

Perhaps you would remember, briefly, just enough to smile at the seriousness of it all.

Then you would step back into the role, letting the world convince you once again — because without forgetting, there could be no surprise, no intimacy, no genuine emotion.
No play. This is what I would do with eternity. I wouldn't stop existing or find some final resting place; I would keep coming back again and again under different circumstances to experience all the thrills that being a conscious being offers.

In this way, remembering and forgetting are not opposites. They are partners.
Each gives meaning to the other. Yin and yang. Duality out of oneness.

The mystery of being alive is not something to solve.
It is something already happening, and you can't stop it.

Nothing essential is missing.
Nothing needs to be achieved.

You are already present.
You always have been.

Presence needs no explanation.

Ask yourself this: can an actor escape the stage
without leaving the play?

Before the first thought
There was already knowing.

Before the question
There was already an answer
that did not need words.

You did not arrive here.
You noticed you were already present.

Who is it
that remembers this?
