

Chapter Seven
The Impossibility of Losing Awareness



 There is a moment, often subtle, when the mind realizes it cannot recognize awareness.
It cannot confirm it.
It cannot secure it.

In that moment, the mind often shifts strategies.
It tries to hold onto the understanding instead.

This holding is quiet.
It is refined.
It feels like care.

But look closely.

What is being held?

A concept.
A memory.
A sense of clarity.
A subtle feeling of openness.

All of these are appearances.
They arise.
They are known.
They fade.

Awareness does not arise or fade.
It does not depend on clarity.
It does not depend on understanding.
It does not depend on memory.

Awareness is not something that can be held because it is not something that can be lost.


  The mind’s fear of losing something essential.
When the mind encounters the simplicity of awareness, it often feels a quiet fear, not dramatic, not emotional, but structural.

The fear is this:

“If I do not hold onto this, it will disappear.”

But look carefully.

What is the “this” that might disappear?

A feeling?
A state?
A sense of spaciousness?
A moment of clarity?

All of these are transient.
They come and go.

Awareness does not.

The fear of losing awareness is the fear of losing a state —
but awareness is not a state.

It is the condition in which all states appear.

The mind cannot lose awareness for the same reason a fish cannot lose water.

It is the medium of experience itself.


  The futility of holding.
Try a simple experiment.

As you read the next sentence, try to hold onto awareness.

Notice what happens.

The mind becomes active.
It tries to maintain a stance.
It tries to preserve a feeling.
It tries to keep something in place.

But awareness is not a feeling.
It is not a stance.
It is not something that can be preserved.

The attempt to hold awareness is like trying to hold the sky.
The hands close on nothing.

This is not failure.
It is clarity.

The mind cannot hold awareness because awareness is not an object.

It is the openness in which holding occurs.


When the mind sees that it cannot hold awareness, it may try to hold the understanding instead.

It may think:

“I know this now.”
“I must remember this.”
“I must stay in this recognition.”

Notice the structure.

Each thought contains a holder,
a subtle “I” who must maintain the insight.

But look carefully.

Where is this holder?

If you search for it, you find only thoughts.
If you look behind the thoughts, you find only awareness.
If you look for the one who is aware, you find nothing at all.

The holder is not an entity.
It is a habit of interpretation.

When this is seen clearly, the holder collapses —
not through effort,
not through surrender,
but through understanding.

Nothing replaces it.
Nothing needs to.

Awareness remains exactly as it has always been.



  The mind’s last illusion: the need to stay aware.
At this point, the mind may attempt one final strategy:

“I must stay aware.”

This feels noble.
It feels disciplined.
It feels like the right thing to do.

But look closely.

To “stay aware” implies:

that awareness can be left

that awareness can be returned to

that awareness is separate from experience

that someone must maintain it

Each implication is false.

You cannot leave awareness because you cannot leave experience.
You cannot return to awareness because you never departed.
You cannot maintain awareness because awareness is not an activity.

The idea of “staying aware” is the last illusion of control.

When this illusion dissolves, nothing dramatic happens.
There is no breakthrough.
No shift.
No attainment.

There is only the quiet recognition that awareness has never depended on you.

What remains when even understanding is not held?


As this chapter settles, notice something simple.

You are not holding awareness.
You are not maintaining awareness.
You are not producing awareness.

Awareness is simply present.

Thoughts appear.
Sensations appear.
Emotions appear.
Understanding appears.

Awareness does not add anything to them.
Awareness does not remove anything from them.

It simply allows them to be known.

This allowing is not an action.
It is not a skill.
It is not a practice.

It is the nature of experience itself.

Nothing needs to be held.
Nothing can be lost.
Nothing needs to be maintained.

Awareness is not something you have.
It is what you are —
not as an identity,
but as the simple fact that anything appears at all.


  As we transition from chapter seven into chapter eight
something may already be apparent.
Not as a conclusion, not as an insight, but as a quiet recognition that awareness cannot be held, cannot be lost, and cannot be maintained.

When the mind sees this, even faintly, it often responds in a new way.
It may begin to interpret the simplicity of awareness as a particular experience,
a sense of openness,
a feeling of spaciousness,
a subtle calm,
a gentle clarity.

These experiences feel meaningful.
They feel connected to the truth.
They feel like signs of progress.

But they are simply experiences,
appearing, changing, fading.

Chapter eight turns toward this movement, not to dismiss these experiences, but to reveal their nature.
