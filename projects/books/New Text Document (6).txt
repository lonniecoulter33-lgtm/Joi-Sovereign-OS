Chapter One
The Dance of Lila
Who Am I, and Why Am I Alive?

There is a question that arises.

It doesn’t always arrive as a sentence. Sometimes it shows up as a pressure beneath the day, a faint insistence that doesn’t match whatever is happening on the surface.

Why am I here?

Not merely here in this room, or in this body, or in this particular set of circumstances—here at all. Here, as the one who is aware of any of it.

For most people, the question doesn’t announce itself. It hides inside ordinary moments. It can appear while washing dishes, while driving in silence, while waking at night with no obvious reason. It doesn’t demand attention. It waits.

Under it there is another question, older and harder to hold without turning it into an idea.

Why is there anything at all?

Not why the world is arranged the way it is, or why events unfold as they do, but why there is experience rather than absence. Why appearance rather than nothing. Why this moment, instead of none.

The mind reaches for answers. It wants an explanation that closes the door. It wants a story that makes the strangeness feel normal.

But this question doesn’t resolve cleanly. It either stays alive, or it gets replaced.

If it is replaced, the replacement is usually an idea: a belief, a philosophy, a conclusion. Something the mind can hold as a possession.

If it stays alive, it behaves differently. It moves through the body. It alters the way the day feels. It makes the familiar look slightly unreal—not because anything has changed, but because the frame has shifted.

The question does not arise in thought alone. It appears as the weight of being here. You don’t think your way into it. You notice you are already inside it.

Who is it that is here?

Not a name. Not a history. Not a role.

Those things exist, and they matter in the world of the play. But the question is not satisfied by them. When you look closely, identity begins to feel assembled rather than discovered—useful, functional, and constantly maintained.

And yet something is undeniably present.

You cannot locate it precisely. You cannot step outside of it to examine it. Whatever you point to—body, thought, emotion, memory—already appears within the very field you are trying to understand.

Different traditions approached this from different angles.

Vedanta asked what remains when everything that can be observed is set aside.

Buddhism asked whether anything stable can be found at all.

Zen refused to let the question settle long enough for the mind to turn it into a conclusion.

Different approaches, the same disturbance in the ordinary sense of things.

The purpose here is not to adopt a lineage, or to collect explanations. The purpose is to look directly, without dressing the looking up as knowledge.

Because something is already happening.

You are reading these lines.

Words appear. Meaning forms. Attention moves from one sentence to the next.

And all of this is known.

Not because it is analyzed, but because awareness is present. The knowing is not created by thought. Thought is one of the things being known.

There is an observer that does not need to be introduced. It is already operating. It does not arrive late. It is present before the sentence completes.

This is not mystical. It is simple.

If you close the book, the knowing remains.

If you keep reading, it remains.

If the mind becomes busy, it remains.

The mind may be loud. The mind may be quiet. The observer does not take on those qualities. It does not flutter. It does not rush.

It watches.

Always present.

Like a child absorbed in a film—fully immersed in the story while never entering the screen. Moved, but not consumed.

This is where the word “Lila” becomes useful—not as doctrine, but as a lens.

Lila is often described as the divine play. Not as a game in the shallow sense, but as a reality that appears, performs itself, and vanishes, while something remains unchanged in the background of it all.

The play includes pain. It includes joy. It includes confusion and clarity. It includes the whole range of human seriousness.

And yet, beneath the seriousness, there is something that never becomes a character.

Long before this body appeared, something was already present—before memory could form, before language could claim ownership.

Long after this body dissolves, something will remain. Not as a personal continuation. Not as a story. As presence without identity. The same plain fact that is here now.

This is not an idea to adopt. It is not a belief to defend.

It requires no agreement.

You do not need to accept it for it to be true.

You may have learned to call yourself many things: a name, a background, a set of traits, a role in a family, a place in society.

Those labels help the story function. They make the world navigable. They are not wrong.

But beneath every role is the same unmoving presence.

Unchanged by success or failure.

Unchanged by praise or blame.

Unchanged by joy or grief.

The stage does not celebrate or mourn.

Characters do.

Masks do.

An actor changes masks across scenes, but the stage remains. The actor does not disappear just because the costume changes.

Now consider the strange privilege of being a conscious being at all.

If you had all of eternity in which to exist, what would you do?

Would you rush toward some final conclusion—some permanent heaven, some final nirvana—never to return? Never to feel love or pain again? Never to be surprised again?

Or would you return, again and again, slipping back into the costume, stepping under the lights, letting the world convince you once more?

Perhaps you would forget yourself completely so the story could feel real. So each scene could matter. So love could captivate you again. So loss could sting again. So laughter could be possible again.

Perhaps you would remember briefly—just enough to smile at the seriousness of it all.

Then you would step back into the role and let the world convince you again.

In that sense, remembering and forgetting are not opposites. They are the movement of the play itself. One gives meaning to the other. Yin and yang. Duality appearing inside oneness.

The mystery of being alive is not something to solve.

It is something already happening, and you cannot stop it.

Nothing essential is missing.

Nothing needs to be achieved.

You may notice that something is already present, without you having arrived anywhere.

Presence needs no explanation.

Before the first thought, there was already knowing.

Before the question, there was already an answer that did not need words.

You did not arrive here.

You noticed you were already present.

Who is it that remembers this?

And if the play is real enough to absorb you, is the stage ever absent?

Ask yourself this:

Can an actor escape the stage without leaving the play?
