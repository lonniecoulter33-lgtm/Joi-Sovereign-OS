.
Chapter Twelve
No Distance to Cross


  There is a moment, often quiet, when the mind realizes it cannot hold a position.
It cannot maintain a view.
It cannot secure an understanding.

In that moment, something unexpected may arise:

A sense of closeness.
A sense of intimacy.
A sense of being near awareness.
A sense of resting in awareness.

These experiences feel profound.
They feel true.
They feel like the essence of the path.

But look closely.

Each of these experiences has a texture.
A quality.
A beginning.
A duration.
An end.

Anything that begins and ends is not fundamental.

The sense of closeness is an experience.
The sense of intimacy is an experience.
The sense of resting is an experience.

Awareness is not an experience.
It is the knowing of experience.


The mind is relational by nature.
It relates to objects.
It relates to people.
It relates to thoughts.
It relates to emotions.
It relates to itself.

When the mind encounters awareness, it tries to relate to that too.

It may think:

“I am close to awareness.”
“I am resting in awareness.”
“I am aligned with awareness.”
“I am one with awareness.”

These thoughts feel refined.
They feel spiritual.
They feel intimate.

But they are still relationships,
and relationships imply distance.

Look carefully.

Where is the distance?

If awareness is the knowing of experience,
and the thought “I am close to awareness” is an experience,
then the thought is known in awareness.

There is no “I” outside awareness to be close or far.

The relationship collapses.


  Try a simple observation.

Notice whatever is present right now.

Now notice the knowing of it.

Is the knowing near the experience?
Is it far from the experience?
Is it inside the experience?
Is it outside the experience?

The question does not apply.

Knowing is not spatial.
It has no distance.
It has no location.
It has no boundary.

The sense of nearness is an interpretation,
a subtle attempt to preserve separation.

When this is seen clearly, nearness dissolves.

Not through effort,
but through understanding.

This is the end of the relationship.
When the mind sees that it cannot be close to awareness,
it may try to be one with awareness.

This feels like the final step.
It feels like unity.
It feels like completion.

But look carefully.

The thought “I am one with awareness” appears.
It is known.
It fades.

The thought is not awareness.
The feeling is not awareness.
The unity is not awareness.

Awareness is the knowing of all of these.

Unity is an experience.
Oneness is an experience.
Nonduality is an experience.

Awareness is not an experience.

It is the openness in which all experiences, including unity, appear.

When this is seen clearly, even unity loses its authority.

Not because it is rejected,
but because it is understood.



  You are not close to awareness.
You are not far from awareness.
You are not in awareness.
You are not outside awareness.
You are not one with awareness.
You are not separate from awareness.

These are all relationships,
and awareness is not relational.

Experience appears.
Awareness knows it.
No distance is crossed.

This is not a state.
It is not an insight.
It is not an achievement.

It is the natural condition of awareness,
distance-free, boundary-free, relation-free.

Nothing needs to be approached.
Nothing needs to be united.
Nothing needs to be dissolved.

There is no distance to cross,
and nothing is lost in this.


  As this chapter settles, something may already be softening.
The sense of nearness may dissolve.
The sense of intimacy may dissolve.
The sense of unity may dissolve.
The sense of resting “in” awareness may dissolve.

What remains is simple:
experience appearing,
awareness knowing,
no distance anywhere.

When the mind sees this, even faintly, it often makes one final attempt to regain orientation.
It may try to understand the absence of distance.
It may try to conceptualize it.
It may try to articulate it.
It may try to grasp it.

This movement is subtle.
It feels intelligent.
It feels precise.
It feels like the final refinement of clarity.

But it is simply the mind trying to convert openness into knowledge.

Chapter thirteen turns toward this movement, not to oppose understanding, but to reveal the freedom of not needing it.
