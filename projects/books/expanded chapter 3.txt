Chapter Three

The Flow of the Play

There is a current running beneath every scene.

It does not announce itself. It does not argue or persuade. It simply moves—quietly, relentlessly—carrying each moment into the next with a precision that no plan could match.

This movement is so constant that it becomes invisible. Like the hum beneath sound, it is noticed only when attention slows or resistance loosens. And yet, without it, no scene could progress, no story could unfold.

Some have called this movement the Tao, destiny, grace, synchronicity, and even providence. Others refuse to name it at all. It has been said that the Tao that can be spoken of is not the real Tao. Words can only point to that which they describe; they can never be that, and so it is with thoughts.

 But names are secondary. What matters is that you have felt it.


You have felt it on days when life seems to arrange itself without effort—when one conversation opens into another, when an obstacle becomes a doorway, when something arrives just as the need for it becomes clear. The timing feels exact, almost scripted, as though the stage itself were paying attention.

In these moments, action does not feel forced. Choices seem to arise already shaped. Even uncertainty carries a strange sense of rightness, as if the scene knows where it is going without explanation.

This is synchronicity showing you the flow of the play.
When events align within awareness,
They are not signs of favor, and they are not rewards. They are not evidence that things are going “right.” They are simply what the play feels like when resistance stops, and movement is allowed to flow. This is becoming one with the Tao in Taoist philosophy.

Nothing special has been added to the scene. Nothing magical has intervened. Only the friction has eased enough for the current to be felt.

Remember that you do not create the current.

But the actor is sometimes carried by it and in these moments you become swept up and taken down the river.
This does not diminish the role. It places it in context. The character still speaks, chooses, responds—but does so from within a movement already in progress.

Consider how little of any moment is actually chosen. The breath arrives. Sensations appear. Sounds enter your awareness without invitation. A thought rises unplanned, unasked for, and demands your attention with it. Before you decide to think, thinking is already happening to you. 

Even intention arrives as a kind of event, surfacing within awareness rather than being summoned from outside it.
The scene advances on its own.
The illusion of control is convincing, though. The character believes it is steering the story, choosing its direction, managing outcomes. This belief is not foolish; it is functioning as a way for the play to remain a mystery. Without it, the role could not be played sincerely.

The character must believe in its agency to care about consequences, to love, to try, to risk. The illusion is not an error to correct, but a tool of the performance.

But beneath the effort to direct the play, something else is always at work.

Like a river moving toward the sea, the story unfolds according to conditions far beyond the character’s reach. Gravity, terrain, weather, time. The water does not debate its course. It meets each obstacle and adjusts, shaping itself to what is present, always flowing along the path of least resistance.

It does not resist the rocks. It includes them.

To flow is not to surrender.

It is to stop pretending to be separate from what is already happening.

Life remains, but it is no longer rigid. It becomes responsiveness rather than control.
When the actor resists the movement and when the mind insists the scene should be different, the play becomes heavy. Lines feel forced. Timing falters. Each step requires effort, justification, and explanation.

The resistance is not wrong. It is part of the scene. But it adds weight where none is required.

But when resistance loosens, something remarkable occurs: the next action appears on its own.
Not as certainty. Not as prophecy. Simply as the obvious next line.
There is often surprise here, not because the action is dramatic, but because it feels so natural once it arrives.

You have experienced this without noticing. A decision made “without thinking.” A word spoken before the mind could rehearse it. A turn taken instinctively that later reveals its necessity.

In hindsight, these moments often feel inevitable, as though the story could not have unfolded any other way. And they couldn't have.

The body knows how to move through the scene.

The mind, however, wants guarantees.

It wants to know where the story leads, how it ends, and whether the effort will be worth it. It seeks a map of the river while standing in the water.

But the river does not hand out maps or travel guides.
It provides movement. Movement provides life's unfolding.

Wait here for a moment. Stop. Take a breath in awareness. Do you hear anything happening around you? 

Notice that this moment is already in motion. The eyes scan the page. The breath rises and falls. Sounds arrive from the surrounding world. None of this waits for instruction.
Even the sense of “pausing” is something that happens rather than something done.
Even attention moves on its own as it is drawn into a phrase, a pause, a memory sparked by a line just read.

What, exactly, is doing all of this?

The more closely this is examined, the less solid the director becomes. Decisions appear, but no decision maker can be found apart from the decision itself. Actions happen, but the actor seems to arise with them, not before them.

This is not a mystical claim. It is an ordinary observation.

The play moves first. The explanation comes later. This is the perceiving of the phenomenal world as it appears before us.

Meaning is added after the fact, woven into memory and narrative. The movement itself does not wait for interpretation.
Synchronicity is simply the moment when this becomes obvious and when the cause and effect loosen their rigid sequence and reveal a deeper coordination. The phone rings at the moment of remembering. The book falls open to the needed passage. A chance meeting redirects the entire plot.

A revelation occurs. You remember.

These moments do not break the rules of the play. They reveal rules that were always operating unnoticed.
These moments feel meaningful because they reveal something that is always true. The actor has never been outside the play.
You are not navigating life from the outside.
You are life, navigating itself.

This recognition does not elevate the character above the scene. It dissolves the imagined distance between them.

This does not mean everything will feel smooth. Rivers will always contain rapids and still water. There will be scenes of confusion, loss, delay, and apparent stagnation. The flow does not promise comfort or an easy way out.

It promises coherence.
Even pain fits. Even disruption belongs, and even what feels like a derailment is part of the larger movement.

 All resistance belongs to the current. Even doubt is carried by it. And ultimately, even the attempt to stop flowing becomes another movement within the same unfolding of the Tao, the great way of the underlying mystery.

Nothing is excluded.

There is no wrong scene.

There is only the scene that is happening. Remember that you are always part of the scene, no matter what is happening around you, because it includes you. You are always on the path; there are no wrong steps in life, only the steps you take.

The mind may protest this. It may insist on alternatives of how things should have gone if only you had done something, or that the life that might have been would have unfolded differently if only different choices had been made. These imagined branches feel vivid, painful, seductive.

They, too, feel like scenes—charged with emotion, urgency, regret.

But notice how they, too, appear as thoughts arriving now, in this moment, as part of the same play.

Nothing escapes the stage.

The freedom, then, is not in controlling the story.
It is in recognizing that you are not required to.

That the weight lifts not because the story ends, but because the burden of authorship and responsibility loosens.
The current does not need your belief. It moves regardless. But when the actor stops bracing against it, the effort softens, and participation becomes lighter, more responsive, more alive.
Action continues, but strain vanishes.

The play does not lead toward a final revelation or a permanent state of clarity. There is no concluding scene where everything resolves, and the movement stops.
There is only this again and again for eternity.

A moment arising. A role responding. A story continues. The eternal cosmic play is always unfolding before your eyes, not some other actor.

The river does not seek the ocean.

It is already moving as it.

So it is with you.

You do not need to find the way.
The way is already unfolding through your steps.

Practice the art of not doing. Doing nothing. This is called wu wei.

Let the next line arrive when it arrives.
Let the next scene reveal itself in its own time.

The actor moves.
The story advances.
And behind it all, the silent watcher remains always
unmoved, unhurried, and always witnessing the flow.

You are never separate from it.
You are never left behind.
