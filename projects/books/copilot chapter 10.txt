
Chapter Ten 
Nowhere to Stand


  There is a moment, often quiet, when the mind stops interpreting experience and simply rests in what is present.
In that moment, something unexpected may arise:

A sense of disorientation.
A sense of groundlessness.
A sense of not knowing where awareness is.
A sense of having nowhere to stand.

This is not confusion.
It is not a problem.
It is not a failure.

It is the natural consequence of seeing that awareness cannot be recognized, held, or interpreted.

When all strategies fall away, the mind looks for a place to stand.
It looks for a center.
It looks for a ground.
It looks for a location.

But awareness is not located.

It has no center.
It has no boundary.
It has no position.

This chapter turns toward the quiet recognition that there is nowhere to stand and that nothing is lost in this.

  
  The Mind’s Final Orientation Reflex
The mind is accustomed to orientation.
It knows where the body is.
It knows where objects are.
It knows where thoughts seem to arise.
It knows where emotions seem to be felt.

Orientation is how the mind navigates the world.

But awareness is not an object in the world.
It is not inside the body.
It is not outside the body.
It is not behind experience.
It is not beneath experience.
It is not above experience.

It is not located at all.

When the mind tries to locate awareness, it is trying to apply spatial logic to something that is not spatial.

This is why the attempt feels confusing.
It is not because awareness is hidden.
It is because the question does not apply.


Try a simple observation.

Notice whatever is present right now.
A sensation.
A sound.
A thought.
A feeling.

Now look for the center of awareness.

Where is the point from which experience is known?

If you look toward the head, you find sensations.
If you look toward the chest, you find sensations.
If you look behind the eyes, you find sensations.
If you look “within,” you find thoughts or feelings.

But you never find a center.

You find only appearances.

The sense of a center is not an observation.
It is an inference, a habit of interpretation.

When this is seen clearly, the center dissolves.
Not through effort,
but through understanding.


When the center dissolves, something subtle may arise:

A sense of groundlessness.

This groundlessness is not instability.
It is not danger.
It is not a loss.

It is the absence of a false ground.

The mind may try to resist this.
It may try to reestablish a center.
It may try to create a new ground.
It may try to stabilize the openness.

But notice:

The attempt to stabilize openness is itself an appearance.
It arises.
It is known.
It fades.

Awareness does not need stability.
It does not need ground.
It does not need orientation.

It is the openness in which all orientations appear.

  
  The freedom of having nowhere to stand.
When the mind stops trying to locate awareness, something becomes clear:

Awareness is not a place.
It is not a position.
It is not a point of view.

It is the absence of position.
The absence of location.
The absence of ground.

This absence is not emptiness.
It is not void.
It is not nothingness.

It is freedom.

Freedom from needing to stand anywhere.
Freedom from needing to locate anything.
Freedom from needing to secure anything.

Experience appears.
Awareness knows it.
Nothing more is required.


You are not standing anywhere.
You are not located anywhere.
You are not positioned in relation to awareness.

Experience is present.
Awareness is present.
No center is found.
No ground is needed.

This is not a state.
It is not an insight.
It is not an achievement.

It is the natural condition of awareness,
open, centerless, groundless, free.

Nothing needs to be held.
Nothing needs to be located.
Nothing needs to be secured.

There is nowhere to stand,
and nothing is lost in this.
