Chapter Seven
Intermission

Imagine realizing you played your role so well that even you believed it.

You forgot it was a role.
Everyone did.

That is how convincing the performance was.

There were no obvious seams. No visible stage directions. The role unfolded from the inside, moment by moment, until it felt indistinguishable from being itself.

The emotions felt real. The stakes felt real. The joys and losses moved you because they were meant to. A great actor does not stand apart from the story. A great actor disappears into it. And this one did completely.

So completely that the line between character and being vanished.

There was no distance left from which to observe. No safe place outside the script. The laughter, the fear, the longing, all of it was lived, not performed.

And then, something new occurred.

Not on the stage but in the audience.

In the ancient Greek theater, this was called theatron — not a building, but the place of seeing. The play did not exist without the witness. The actor required the gaze, just as the gaze required the actor. Even then, the mystery was not the story, but the seeing of it.

The ones watching began to recognize something familiar. A gesture. A voice. A feeling they had known intimately. Slowly, quietly, they realized they were not merely observers of the play.

What they recognized was themselves. You.

They had been performing too.

The audience looks around.
Seat by seat.
Row by row.

Go ahead and look around.

At first, the recognition is subtle, almost disorienting. A sense that something does not quite add up. A flicker of awareness that the boundary between watcher and watched is thinner than assumed.

The ones sitting beside them were also on the stage. The seats themselves were props. The walls are part of the set. The lights, the script, the timing, everything belonged to the same production.

Nothing existed outside of it.

There was never a divide.

Only perspective.

And in that recognition, the play pauses, not to end, but to breathe



Let's take an Intermission.

Not a stopping point.
A resting point.

It has been a very long show.

How long? Well, since before any beginning. Eternity.
So go ahead and stand up.

Stretch your legs.
Feel the body again.
Get a drink.

Walk through the theater that is also the stage. Intermingle with your fellow actors, some friends, some family, others seemingly strangers, all cast in the same cosmic play, all having forgotten and remembered in their own time.

Some are still deep in character. Some are just beginning to question the script. Some are mid-remembrance, unsure whether to laugh or cry.

No hierarchy.
No lead role.
No extras.

Every part is essential. No scene is complete without each one.

The quiet background figure shapes the story as much as the one under the spotlight. The pauses matter as much as the dialogue. The forgetting is as necessary as the remembering.

In Hindu thought, this was called Lila — the divine play. Not a mistake. Not a test. But a creative expression where the One becomes many, just long enough to experience itself.

The show will continue.

It always has.

There was never a true interruption, only moments of awareness folding back into movement.

But something has shifted.

This time, it is not one actor taking a bow while the audience applauds from the dark. This time, the entire theater stands together. The boundary between the stage and the seats dissolves with the recognition that there never truly was a boundary.

All of existence is participating.

No one remains unseen. Not even you.

The audience bows.

The actors bow.

They are one and the same. You should also bow if you haven't already.

This is group consciousness elevated not by effort, but by recognition. A shared remembering that everyone is inside the scene, and the scene is incomplete without you.

Not a mass awakening.
Not a dramatic event.
But that a quiet realignment is spreading naturally, one recognition at a time.

Yes, you.

The one reading this now.
The one looking out from behind your eyes.
The true you.
The actor who never left the stage.

You were never late to the performance.
You never missed your cue.

You earned this.

Not by perfection.
Not by understanding.
Not by escaping the play.

But by playing your role fully, so fully that you forgot, and then remembered.

That forgetting was not a failure.

It was part of the story's intimacy. And it helps carry the punchline.

Go ahead.

Take your bow.

Not as an ending.
As an acknowledgment.

You remember now why you are here?

You know that you have always been just right where you are,
Always playing your part on the stage.

The question of who I am now falls away like leaves dropping in the fall, soon to be swept away by the next breeze.

With recognition that you are who you have always been through every scene in every moment of your eternal life.

The silent observer, the witness, the actor, the parent, the child, the lover, the mask wearer. This is you.

Nothing needs to be carried forward except this gentle orientation — a willingness to return again and again to simple presence while fully living the role.

The lights dim slightly.
The set remains.
This is a story without an end.

Intermission is over.

Welcome back to the stage.


And with that, the show must go on, and so the play continues.
